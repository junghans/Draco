Conclusion

As I concluded in my prior article[1], these are exciting days for C++. With the ongoing maturation of the standard, the increasing availability of high quality compilers as well as implementations of the C++ standard library, increasing facility among C++ programmers, and a user base that is growing at a phenomenal rate both inside and outside the scientific community, we are faced with unique opportunities for rewriting the future of scientific computing in C++. Only a short while ago, it seemed an inescapable conclusion that C++ would be forever daunted by poor numeric performance and an exasperating dearth of quality reusable scientific subroutine libraries. Indeed the whole concept of reusable scientific software seemed to have been rendered absurd by the explosion of user defined containers.

Today we can confidently declare that these early assessments have been overturned. Expression templates have beaten the curse of overloading binary operators. Glommability has made this technology accessible to all containers. And generic programming interfaces combined with traits enable the expression of container-free numerical algorithms in C++.

The fundamental conclusion is that the construction of high quality, reusable scientific algorithms is possible. It simply has to be practiced.
